Care Support Options

There are several options you may consider for meeting your loved one’s care needs, including services and support that may be available for free, arranging residential care, getting help at home from a paid carer, sharing care among family and friends, supporting your loved one’s independent living or moving in with your loved one to make it easier to provide daily support.

The right option or combination of them will depend on your individual circumstances.

What is the range of options for meeting my loved one’s care needs?

There's some help and support that people with care needs can get for free no matter what their income is, including some equipment and home adaptations, help after coming home from hospital, NHS continuing healthcare and nursing in a care home. Find out more on the NHS page about free social care and support.

Residential care

There are different types of residential care that you, your loved one requiring care and your family may consider. Below are links to useful information on these options:

  • NHS page on care homes
  • Age UK page on assisted living
  • Age UK page on sheltered or retirement housing

Getting help from a paid carer

There are ways to get professional care services at home, such as hiring a paid carer. You can visit the NHS website to learn more about when to consider help at home from a paid carer and how to arrange it. Visit GOV.UK page on employing someone in your home to learn about your responsibilities if you choose to hire a paid carer directly, rather than through an agency.

Sharing care with family and friends

Caring for a loved one with health needs often requires teamwork and this may mean relying on each other for support and sharing care activities. It is important you decide on who will be responsible for which tasks.

There are aspects you should consider when sharing care with family and friends:

  • You may find that it is best to first allocate a primary caregiver. That way the primary caregiver can step in if there is a crisis
  • You may find that it’s best to discuss each other’s strengths and skills and then agree in advance how your efforts can complement one another so you play to each other’s strengths and become an effective team
  • You may need to use the PREPARE acronym from our page on Planning Ahead, to help you consider how much you can actually do.

Supporting your loved one’s independent living

If supporting your loved one’s independent living is the best option for you, there are some things that may help, including home adaptations, mobility equipment, household gadgets and monitoring systems. Find out more information on the NHS webpages linked below:

  • Home adaptations and home assessment
  • Mobility equipment
  • Household gadgets and equipment
  • Personal alarms, monitoring systems and key safes

Sharing your home with your loved one requiring care

If your loved one requiring care doesn’t live with you, the time may come when you start thinking about sharing your home with them. This might be because they’ll need more care in the future if their health gets worse, or they’ve recently come out of hospital after an illness or fall. Visit the NHS website for advice on sharing your home.

What support may be available for financing care?

  • Support from local council through the Needs Assessment: This assessment will help to identify what type of support your loved one needs, how this support will be given and how the local council can help. You can arrange a needs assessment by contacting your loved one’s local council. Find our more information on the NHS page on getting a social care needs assessment
  • Financial support for your loved one with health needs: Your loved one may be entitled to the following financial support
    • Personal Independence Payment (PIP) can help with extra living costs and is available to those who have both a long-term physical or mental health condition or disability and difficulty doing certain everyday tasks or getting around because of their condition. PIP is available even for those who are working, have savings or are getting most other benefits
    • Attendance Allowance helps with extra costs and is available to those who have a disability severe enough that they need someone to help look after them. It’s paid at 2 different rates and how much a person gets depends on the level of care that they need because of their disability

How to consider my own needs, when weighing these options?

Considering your own needs is important when thinking about what role you can play in providing support and care for your loved one. We have created the acronym PREPARE, which you can find on our page on Planning Ahead to help you consider your situation and needs, when thinking about how you can best support your loved one.

How to discuss the options with my family, relative who requires care and healthcare professionals?

You may find yourself having to discuss care options with family, relative who requires care and healthcare professionals. This may be challenging, or even a new experience for you. We have provided conversation tips for discussing options with family and relative who requires care.

Carers UK created a guide to help people who are caring after their loved ones to have effective conversations with professionals and advocate for themselves. You can access this guide on the Carers UK website.
